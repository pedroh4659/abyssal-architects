Crust

Iara, a planet with impressive characteristics, was born from the agglutination of cosmic material in a distant solar system. With a radius of approximately 6,371 km and a mass of 3.24963×10¹⁵ kg, its structure began to solidify into a newborn planet. During the first millennia, Iara's crust was formed predominantly from basalt, the result of a massive magma cooling process. Its crust was rich in sediments, composed of mineral particles, remnants of the intense volcanic activity that shaped its surface.

Rain began to fall.

After millions of years, Iara's humid and moderate atmosphere began to accumulate water vapor. Colossal storm clouds formed, heavy with moisture. The atmosphere, still dense with water vapor, reflected the light from its home star, Winston. The red dwarf, billions of years older than Iara, illuminated the young planet, which had formed within its thin habitable zone. The water, born from the planet's igneous exhalations, nurtured the life that began to spread on Iara.

Not for days or weeks, but for thousands of years, torrential rains hammered the young Iara, filling vast basins and smoothing the jagged landscape. These relentless rains pooled and gathered, forming Iara's oceans.

The skies darkened as colossal storm clouds gathered, heavy with moisture. An electrifying tension filled the air as the first raindrops fell. But these were no ordinary rains; they were colossal torrents, pouring down in an unending deluge. For eons, these storms pummeled the surface, sculpting the land and filling the myriad basins that dotted the planet’s surface.

As the rain fell and Iara cooled, vast oceans emerged, shimmering under a sky still thick with steam. The land, though still young and volatile, began to settle around these newborn seas. Iara was no longer a world of fire but one of water. It was this water, formed from the fiery breath of the planet itself, that would one day cradle the spark of life.
